Welcome Screen

First impression and conversion moment. Goal: communicate value, signal tone, reduce anxiety about starting something new. Every word earns its place.

1
Headline
"You don't have to figure it out alone."
Addresses the primary emotional barrier to adoption: the feeling that needing help is a personal failure. 'Alone' reframes the app as companionship, not surveillance or data collection. Avoids product feature claims ("the #1 student wellness app") in favor of emotional truth.
2
Subhead
"Peak helps you stay on track, check in with yourself, and find support when you need it."
Three parallel verbs (stay, check in, find) communicate product scope without an overwhelming feature list. Serial structure creates rhythm and scanability. No exclamation points — wellness context requires calm, not excitement.
3
CTAs
"Get started" / "Already have an account? Sign in"
Primary CTA is direct without manufactured urgency. Secondary is a complete sentence (not just "Sign in") because it answers the user's question before they have to ask. No visual hierarchy that guilts the user into the primary path.

Key Design Decisions

  • No exclamation points anywhere on this screen
  • Headline targets emotional state, not product features
  • Secondary CTA is equally accessible — no small gray text
  • Logo mark present but not dominant — product earns attention, not the brand

Goals Selection Screen

High-friction onboarding moment. Users are being asked to categorize their struggles before they trust the app. Every content decision reduces commitment anxiety and increases completion.

1
Progress Indicator
Unlabeled progress bar (no step count)
Research consistently shows that numbered steps ("Step 3 of 7") increase onboarding abandonment. Users calculate remaining effort and disengage. An unlabeled bar communicates progress without creating a countdown. The bar is thin and unobtrusive — it doesn't compete with the task.
2
Headline
"What's on your plate right now?"
Familiar idiom normalizes the presence of multiple struggles. Contrast with "Tell us about yourself" (surveillance framing) or "Select your goals" (clinical/formal). "Right now" anchors to the present moment — this isn't about life goals, it's about today.
3
Subhead
"You can always change this later."
Critical phrase. Reduces perceived permanence of the choice. Users who fear "locking in" an answer are more likely to abandon or answer dishonestly. This single sentence increases both completion rates and response quality.
4
Primary CTA
"This looks right" / "Skip for now"
"This looks right" asks for light confirmation, not commitment. "Next" implies forward momentum without reflection; "This looks right" invites the user to review their selections before proceeding. "Skip for now" is genuinely offered — same size, same position, not buried. Respecting the skip reduces resentment and increases long-term engagement.

Key Design Decisions

  • Multi-select with no minimum — users can select zero items if needed
  • Options are neutral, not clinical ("stress or anxiety" not "mental health struggles")
  • "Something else" always included — users who don't see themselves disengage
  • Selected state uses visual weight, not color alone (WCAG accessibility)

Home Dashboard

Highest-exposure screen in the app. A user who checks in daily sees this hundreds of times a semester. Every content decision compounds. Nothing can feel rote, pushy, or clinical.

1
Greeting
"Good morning / Hey, Jordan."
Two-line greeting: time-sensitive context line ("Good morning") followed by personal name with casual register ("Hey"). "Hey" signals friendship register, not institutional register. Uses the name the user gave in onboarding — whatever they said, including nicknames. "Jordan Smith" would be institutional; "Jordan" is human.
2
Check-in Card
"How are you feeling today? No right answer here. Takes about 2 minutes."
"No right answer here" defuses performance anxiety — students in wellness apps often report checking in based on what they think they should feel, not what they do. "Takes about 2 minutes" sets expectations and reduces the commitment barrier. Time estimates in CTAs consistently improve conversion on high-effort actions.
3
Deadline Display
"2 days" (urgent) vs "Next week" (low urgency)
Urgency is signaled through visual weight (bold, darker color for urgent items) — not through alarm language. No "DUE SOON!" or "Don't forget!" The fact alone is sufficient. Users who are already stressed do not need the app to amplify their anxiety through exclamatory copy.
4
Resource Card
"Campus Counseling Center — Now offering evening hours"
Resource notifications lead with the most useful fact (evening hours) rather than "New resource available." The subtitle answers "why does this matter to me right now?" before the user has to ask. No "Might be useful!" — the exclamation point would trivialize access to mental health services.

Key Design Decisions

  • Streak displayed as a neutral fact ("3-day streak"), not a gamification threat
  • Four navigation tabs cover the full product surface without overwhelming
  • Check-in card is first — the product's primary value proposition leads
  • Resource card uses no assumption of need ("Might be useful" vs "You need this")
