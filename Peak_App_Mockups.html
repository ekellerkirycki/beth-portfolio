Welcome / Onboarding Entry — first impression, emotional tone, conversion moment

Peak

You don't have to figure it out alone.

Peak helps you stay on track, check in with yourself, and find support when you need it.

1
Headline: "You don't have to figure it out alone" — addresses the primary emotional barrier to adoption. Reframes the app as companionship, not surveillance.
2
Subhead: Three parallel verbs (stay on track, check in, find support) communicate scope without overwhelming. No exclamation points.
3
CTAs: "Get started" is direct, not manufactured. Secondary signin is visible without competing — no guilt, no hierarchy pressure.

Screen 2 of 3

Onboarding — Goals Selection — reducing commitment anxiety at a high-friction moment

9:41

What's on your plate right now?

Pick everything that applies. You can always change this later.

Keeping up with coursework
Managing stress or anxiety
Staying motivated
Finding campus resources
Connecting with others
Something else
1
Headline: "What's on your plate" — familiar idiom normalizes struggle. Not "Tell us about yourself" (data-collection framing).
2
Subhead: "You can always change this later" — critical phrase. Reduces perceived permanence and increases completion rates.
3
Primary CTA: "This looks right" — asks for light confirmation, not blind progression. "Skip for now" is genuinely offered, not hidden.
4
Progress bar: Unlabeled. Research shows numbered steps ("Step 3 of 7") increase abandonment. A bar communicates progress without dread.

1
Greeting: "Hey, Jordan" — uses the name they gave us, conversationally. Not "Welcome back, Jordan Smith."
2
Check-in framing: "No right answer here" — defuses performance anxiety. "Takes about 2 minutes" — sets expectations, reduces commitment barrier.
3
Deadlines: "2 days" in red signals urgency without alarm language. No "URGENT!" or "Don't forget!" — just the fact.
4
Resource card: "Now offering evening hours. Might be useful." — leads with the useful fact, low-pressure language, no assumption of need.
