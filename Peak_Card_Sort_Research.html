Background

Why Card Sorting?

Peak's resources section needed to surface 24+ campus support services in a way that matched how students actually think about their needs — not how institutions organize their departments. Card sorting is the foundational method for this kind of information architecture work.

01

Open Card Sort

Participants organize cards into groups they create and name themselves. No predefined categories. Reveals users' mental models and natural language for content groupings.

Mental model discovery
02

Closed Card Sort

Participants sort cards into predefined categories. Tests whether a proposed structure makes sense to users. Validates or challenges existing IA decisions.

Structure validation
03

Hybrid Card Sort

Combines both: predefined categories exist, but participants can create new ones if needed. Balances structure testing with flexibility for edge cases and surprises.

Refinement & synthesis
Research Question

How do college students mentally categorize campus support services — and does that mental model match Peak's proposed four-tab navigation structure (Check-in / Courses / Resources / Community)?

Phase 1
Open Card Sort

Discovering Mental Models

8 participants. No predefined categories. Each participant sorted 30 cards into groups they named themselves. The goal: understand how students naturally organize campus support — before we impose any structure.

Emergent Categories from Participants

Participants created between 4 and 9 categories each. After synthesis, five dominant groupings emerged across participants:

Key Finding

Students organized resources by what they needed to feel better, not by which department provided it. "Get emergency food" went with mental health help more often than with financial services — because students associated it with crisis, not budgeting. This was the most important signal for Peak's IA.

Surprise Finding

"Check my mood history" was consistently sorted into Academics — not Mental Health. Students explained: "That's performance data. Like checking my grades." This challenged the assumption that check-in features belong under a wellness tab.

Phase 2
Closed Card Sort

Validating the Proposed Structure

8 participants (different from Phase 1). Predefined categories based on Peak's initial navigation proposal: Check-in, Courses, Resources, Community. Participants sorted 30 cards into these four tabs — and could flag cards that didn't fit anywhere.

Key Finding

High agreement (80%+) for core items in each tab. But three categories of confusion emerged: (1) Crisis and mental health items split between Resources and Check-in — suggesting students see the check-in as a gateway to help, not just a tracking tool. (2) "Financial aid" didn't belong anywhere — it felt like a different kind of resource. (3) "Peer mentoring" was seen as a Resource by some, a Community feature by others.

Phase 3
Hybrid Card Sort

Refining the Architecture

8 new participants. Predefined categories (revised after Phase 2) plus the ability to create new categories for items that didn't fit. Focused specifically on the 11 cards with under 70% agreement in Phase 2.

Revised Categories Tested

Hypothesis Going In

Based on Phase 1 findings, we hypothesized that students think in terms of urgency as much as topic. "I need help right now" vs "I need to plan ahead." We tested a revised IA that surfaced urgent resources differently from ongoing support.

Hybrid Finding

"Mood history" moved to Academics in 6 of 8 hybrid sessions — confirming the Phase 1 surprise. Students see self-tracking as performance data, not wellness care. The check-in feature stays in the Check-in tab, but the history of check-ins belongs alongside grades and deadlines.

Synthesis & Decisions

What the Research Changed

Three rounds of card sorting produced specific, actionable IA decisions. Here's what changed from the original design — and why.

Before Research

📋 Check-in — mood tracking, wellness tools, crisis line
📚 Courses — grades, deadlines, tutoring, advisor
🙋 Resources — counseling, medical, financial aid, basic needs
👥 Community — study groups, peer mentoring, events

After Research

📋 Check-in — daily mood check-in, breathing tools, reflections
📚 Courses — grades, deadlines, mood history, tutoring, advisor
🙋 Resources — counseling, medical, financial aid, crisis line, basic needs
👥 Community — study groups, events, clubs, peer mentoring

Three Specific Changes Made

Change 1

Mood history moved from Check-in to Courses. Students treat self-tracking as academic performance data. Surfacing it alongside grades reduces friction and increases the feature's perceived utility.

Change 2

Crisis line moved from Check-in to Resources. While the check-in can surface crisis resources contextually, the crisis line as a standalone feature belongs where students go when they need help — not where they go to track how they feel.

Change 3

Peer mentoring moved from Community to Resources. Students see mentoring as a support service they access, not a community they join. The language matters: "Get a peer mentor" belongs with other services; "Find study partners" belongs in Community.

Teaching Artifact

Student Assignment Version

This card sort was designed as a replicable teaching exercise. Students conduct their own version of this study, analyze results, and propose an IA for a fictional product of their choice.

Card Sorting Assignment: Information Architecture for [Your Product]

ENGL 46700/56701 — Writing for Multiple Media | Interaction Design Unit

STEP 01

Define Your Cards

Identify 20–30 content items or features your product needs to surface. Write each in plain language — how a user would describe it, not how a department would label it.

STEP 02

Run an Open Sort

Ask 5–8 participants to group your cards however makes sense to them and name each group. Use index cards physically or OptimalSort digitally. Document every category name.

STEP 03

Synthesize & Propose

Find patterns across participants. Which items clustered consistently? Which caused confusion? Propose an IA based on the dominant mental models you observed.

STEP 04

Validate with Closed Sort

Run a closed sort with your proposed structure. Measure agreement rates. Identify any items with under 70% agreement — those need redesign or reclassification.

STEP 05

Document Your Rationale

Write a 500-word synthesis explaining what you found, what you changed, and why. Cite specific participant behaviors or quotes where possible.

STEP 06

Present Your IA

Present your final navigation structure with agreement data. Be prepared to defend decisions where agreement was mixed — ambiguity is a research finding, not a failure.

Instructor Note

The most valuable learning moment in this exercise is when participants disagree — when 40% put a card in Category A and 60% put it in Category B. That ambiguity is data. It means the content item needs either clearer labeling, a different structural home, or contextual placement in multiple locations. Students who learn to sit with ambiguity rather than forcing a clean answer are learning to think like information architects.
